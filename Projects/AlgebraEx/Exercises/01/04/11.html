  4. Look at $X^2$ with $X$ defined in a.: $$ X^2 = \begin{bmatrix}1& a+a& b+ac+b\\0&1&c+c\\0&0&1 \end{bmatrix} $$ We see that in $\Z/2\Z$, $X^2$ is the identity if and only if $ac = 0$. Thus, we only have two matrices that do not have order 2. Moreover, if $X$ is one of those matrices, $X^2$ is not. Therefore, $X^4$ is the identity and $X$ must have order 4, it cannot have order 3 because it would imply $X$ is the inverse of $X^2$ but we know that $X^2$ is its own inverse.
  5. We still consider the matrix $X$, let $n \in \N$. Suppose $a$ or $c$ is non-zero, then the upper diagonal entries of $X^n$ will be $(na, nc) \neq (0,0)$, so $X^n$ is not the identity. If $a= c=0$, then we must have $b\neq 0$ (otherwise $X$ is the identity) and the corner entry of $X^n$ will be $nb \neq 0$, so $X^n$ is not the identity. Since, there is no integer $n$ such that $X^n$ is the identity, $X$ has infinite order.
