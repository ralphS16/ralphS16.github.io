Chapter 1: #1.6.14

If $x,y \in \ker(\varphi)$, then since $$\varphi(x^{-1}) = \varphi(x)^{-1} = 1^{-1} = 1$$ and $$\varphi(xy) = \varphi(x)\varphi(y) = 1\cdot 1 = 1,$$ we get that both $x^{-1}$ and $xy$ are in the kernel. By 1.1.26, $\ker(\varphi)$ is a subgroup of $G$.

($\Rightarrow$)Suppose $\varphi$ is injecitve, we already know that $1 \in \ker(\varphi)$, suppose $x \neq 1 \in \ker(\varphi)$, then $\varphi(x) = \varphi(1)$ which contradicts the injectiveness of $\varphi$. ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $\ker(\varphi) = \{1\}$ and there exists $x,y \in G$ such that $\varphi(x) = \varphi(y)$. We can rearrange to get $\varphi(xy^{-1}) = 1$. By our hypothesis, $xy^{-1} = 1$ and by uniqueness of the inverse, we must have $x = y$, implying $\varphi$ is injective.
